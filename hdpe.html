Plastic Fabrication

originally shared @ https://lafelabs.github.io/hdpe.html
3d printers are not a path to free technology. If you go buy one at Home Depot, you have to buy a spool of plastic to go with it that costs more than many of the finished products made of much higher quality plastic also available, such as chairs and snow shovels. Then when you print things the quality is extremely low, the software is often frustrating and broken, the thing you make cannot then be remade with that hardware, it must be sent off to some factory for recycling when you're done with it(or more likely buried with everything else). And they're full of patents, making the whole technology fairly broken for anyone looking to make useful things without helping a few technocrats get even richer. And at several hundred dollars minimum cost they're hardly a mass market product, even if rich "tech" people all buy them in droves(and then print 5 things and never use them again).

The opposite extreme for plastics manufacturing in terms of scale is injection molding, by far the most practical means of manufacturing with plastics in bulk. This is very low cost for high volume but insanely high cost at low volume, with a minimum investment for a mold being a few thousand dollars or a few tens of thousands. Making a new mold takes time, energy, skills, and is difficult to automate due to the craft aspects of getting the mold right. It can take a few tries to get the correct mold design which is expensive in both time and energy and money. This is also not relevant for truly free technology for several reasons: scale breaks at small number, it requires high levels of technical expertise, it's energy intensive, requires that high quality metals machining also exist along side it to make the molds, and again cannot be edited after being cast. One can recycle it easily, yes, but not edit or repair an existing part.

The other major technology for manufacturing plastics which is mostly not used at scale but is useful for prototyping is CNC machining. This can make high quality parts fast, but is very expensive, needs heavy fixed infrastructure, is hard to build from trash, and needs a lot of specialized expertise.

My methods of plastic fabrication involve abandoning the paradigms of both additive(3d printing) and subtractive manufacturing(CNC), by converting a feedstock of mixed plastic trash into useful industrial products by cutting(not melting or shredding) them up into standard geometric shapes, then using heat welding and simple manipulation tools like tweezers and pliers to create three dimensional parts from 2 dimensional components. As with 3d printing, this can be used to make either mostly hollow structures or structures reinforced with an arbitrarily high density of structural lattices(typically honeycomb for most 3d printing systems).

I have been experimenting with various plastic trash feedstocks and have found that HDPE and LDPE(high and low density polyethylene) are by far the most tractable with these methods. In theory this can be extended to any thermoplastic, such as PMMA, ABS or PP, but for now I'm using HDPE and LDPE for all work here as those are easy to work with by hand and easy to find both in litter in the environment and in my personal trash feedstock based on the milk I consume daily with my breakfast cereal. Most milk containers are made from HDPE, as well as a lot of bottle caps for PP bottles(which I've found very frustrating to work with but the caps are great). The big orange traffic barriers(not cones) which can often be found trashed in urban swamp areas after they get hit by trucks, smashed and kicked off the highway are also a great source of LDPE.

At present my method is extremely simple: I cut plastic milk bottles up with a box cutter, wash them in a sink cut them into shapes with scissors, then use tweezers and a candle flame to make the weld joints. I developed these methods initially using a hot air gun with temperature control set to 130 C which makes the process much easier but have found that with practice the candle or even a lighter can be used just as well. Milk bottle work all calls for tweezers, and I use very flat bladed ones from the eye makeup section of the grocery store or pharmacy, which are intended for eyelash related work(hence the flat blades).

I have documented some of the processes for building useful stuff with these methods on Instructables and Youtube:

  • tube for fluidics/plumbing
  • Fabricating motor coil using welded HDPE
  • Video of making a motor coil spool from milk bottle
In addition to spools for motor coils, I've experimented with various objects made from HDPE using simple geometry(all from Action Geometry, and to be eventually documented in Geometron) which can be used for plumbing, fluidics, and optics(reflectors and light shields for bike lights and microscope illuminators). I've also made useful bike parts using welded LDPE at a larger scale(a cup holder for my handlebars.)

Unlike 3d printed parts it has proven to be easy to cut up and reform or just make small modifications to existing parts. I have also used HPDE bottle caps as structural elements for electronic circuits, and then integrated those via welds into various motor coil geometries. It's also relatively easy to integrate this into cardboard fabrication techniques from Action Geometry, making various tabs and slots as needed, and welding things into place. Finally I've found that connecting plastic parts with wood is simple without threaded fasteners or nails or wire by cutting holes into sticks and shoving glassy plastic into the holes, which harden into fasteners which can be easily broken off later and reformed.

WHAT IS TRASH MAGIC?

Trash Magic is a game of MUTUAL AID quests. We want to have strong communities and live well. Many of us are suffering from financial challenges, loss of community, and meaningless lifestyles. Consumer-based economics has generated an unfathomable amount of second-hand materials. By taking this opportunity to design useful things out of free materials, we believe that we can live in a society that costs no money to build and maintain. When good things made for free contain instructions so that other people can copy them for free, we replace a culture of ownership and scarcity with a culture of replication and abundance.

Why does every Trash Magic blueprint in our system include these explanations? Reflect on how every cell in a body contains the DNA to replicate that entire body. Any page in this system can be copied and shared. No matter which blueprint you copy or how many, every single one will contain all the necessary instructions for anyone else who stumbles along any single page to not only copy the entire digital network of information, but also to build a free and healthy community in the real world.

This system currently lives at https://dna-server.github.io

We define Trash Magic in 3 ways.

Trash Magic is: (1) good for the community, (2) made for free, and (3) holds instructions to (a) copy, (b) change, and (c) recycle itself

(1) GOOD FOR THE COMMUNITY:
If a helpful thing can be created and used by individuals sustainably without exploitation, or if it is accessible, sustainable, and useful for the community at large, then it is good for the community.

(2) MADE FOR FREE:
Ideally, the thing is upcycled from used materials. If it is sustainably sourced from nature, this is okay too. It's even normal to spend a little money to get basic tools. But if something is too expensive or rare for most people to get, or too complicated for most people to use, then it will not replicate very easily. We want everything that we create to be easy for others to copy, because we love to share and we want everyone to be happy and healthy and have what we have.

We are scaling this system at a "negative marginal cost", which means that the more we make self-replicating stuff out of free stuff (sharing our blueprints openly as we do so), the easier it becomes for everyone else to make self-replicating stuff out of free stuff. We create social networks and communities in which any person helping to build a free society can share their projects with everyone else, so everyone benefits exponentially. For the first time in history, our planet is covered with perfectly good stuff that nobody wants just because it's not "brand new". Everything has already been mined out of the ground, woven, molded, purified, shaped, welded, and otherwise assembled into a useful form. The tools needed to work with these resources are now widespread, as well.

(3) HOLDS INSTRUCTIONS:
Everything we make should be easy to use and copy. The copying instructions or a hyperlink to them should be physically connected to the thing itself. The instructions should be easy to find and understand. The blueprints and assembly should be designed in a way that makes the thing easy to modify and adapt to new situations and needs. It should also contain information on how it can be taken apart without destroying it and how the individual parts can be reused in new projects.

This continual reuse becomes its Lore. The Story of when and how an artifact has been used becomes part of its value. Mythic Quests are fulfilled through Trash Magic. This Story can be infused with the Instructions for further Magick. Imagine how magical it would be to teach The Instructions of replication through Storytelling.

Everything should copy easily because the replication instructions are literally right in your hands. The replication instructions should also hold an overview of the Trash Magic system (just like the one that you're reading now). This allows us to share not only our creations themselves, but the goals of the overarching system of Trash Magic.

You "win" the game when your community has free and open access to a COMPLETE SET, which is a human network that meets all the following physical and social needs:

PHYSICAL HUMAN NEEDS
• Food
• Clean water
• Disposal of human waste
• Tools
• Buildings with Temperature Control: the air of indoor environments should have heat and cooling of some kind, these shelters should be constructed in such a way that this needs minimal...
• Energy (use natural heat and coolness from the environment)
• Communication / networking / controls / automation / audio / video / simulations: these are the real reasons we need “computers”
• Medicine and drugs

SOCIAL HUMAN NEEDS
• Generalized and Specific Social Supports
• Accessibility to participate in and opportunities to contribute to society
• Therapy and Counseling
• Social and communal places, 3rd places, walkable communities, etc.
• Access to Trash Magic Networks, which of course should include all of this

All of these needs count as their own SET. For example, the FOOD SET is a collection of techniques for cultivating, finding, or preparing foods. When you begin building communities and infrastructure in the real world, it might start small. But as you grow, you will soon have more and more items in each SET. Eventually you will be able to provide for these needs without financial challenges, lack of community, or meaningless lifestyles. When all of your SETS are filled with techniques that meet human needs in free, communal, and meaningful ways, you have a COMPLETE SET. Congratulations! Now it's time to RECYCLE and REPLICATE.

Replication Quests plant the seeds of the entire Trash Magic system. Replication Quests are about giving things away for free. You don't need to have a COMPLETE SET to do this. You just have to give something away for free. When the thing has copying instructions attached, anyone who receives it can recreate that particular thing or the entire Trash Magic system. You can also take things apart and spread the separate pieces, making sure that each individual piece contains all the copying instructions. To do this, first make a thing. Then RECYCLE it by detaching it into separate parts and pass them along to different people who you hope will REPLICATE the whole thing for free.

If, for example, the item is made from five parts, then each person who gets one of the five parts can use the copying instructions attached to rebuild the other four parts for free to make their own copy of that item. Another way to do this is to have a team of five people. They assemble to complete a task, then all five go out to find four new members for five new teams. Both of these examples show how to replicate one thing into five. Hyperseeds are any parts of any COMPLETE SET that can evolve itself into a healthy community in the real world by using free parts from its surroundings.
